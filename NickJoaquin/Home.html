Introduction

Nicomedes Joaquín y Márquez (Nick Joaquin)
National Artist for Literature in 1976
Born: May 4, 1917
Died: April 29, 2004

He is regarded by many as the most distinguished Filipino writer in English writing so variedly and so well about so many aspects of the Filipino. Nick Joaquin has also enriched the English language with critics coining “Joaquinesque” to describe his baroque Spanish-flavored English or his reinventions of English based on Filipinisms. Aside from his handling of language, Bienvenido Lumbera writes that Nick Joaquin’s significance in Philippine literature involves his exploration of the Philippine colonial past under Spain and his probing into the psychology of social changes as seen by the young, as exemplified in stories such as Doña Jeronima, Candido’s Apocalypse and The Order of Melchizedek. Nick Joaquin has written plays, novels, poems, short stories and essays including reportage and journalism. As a journalist, Nick Joaquin uses the nom de plume Quijano de Manila but whether he is writing literature or journalism, fellow National Artist Francisco Arcellana opines that “it is always of the highest skill and quality” .

A Brief Story of Nick Joaquin's Life

Life and Family

Nicomedes "Nick" Joaquin y Marquez, also known as "Onching," was born on May 4, 1917, in Paco, Manila. He was the fifth child of Don Leocadio Joaquin and Salomé Marquez. Don Leocadio, a colonel, fought in the Philippine Revolution and retired after being wounded. Salomé Marquez, a well-educated woman, taught in a Manila public school. The Joaquin family lived in a two-story residential and commercial building on Herran Street in Paco, Manila. Joaquin had a happy childhood, being tutored in Spanish and piano and encouraged to have an interest in the arts. The family communicated in Spanish and heard mass regularly.

Don Leocadio lost the family fortune in the late 1920s due to a failed oil exploration project. The family moved out of their Herran home and moved into a rented house in Pasay. Don Leocadio passed away soon after, leaving Joaquin at twelve years old.

Joaquin deeply admired José Rizal, the national hero of the Philippines, paying him tribute in such books as The Storyteller's New Medium: Rizal in Saga, The Complete Poems and Plays of Jose Rizal, and A Question of Heroes: Essays in Criticism on Ten Key Figures of Philippine History. He translated the hero's valedictory poem, in the original Spanish Mi Ultimo Adios, as "Land That I Love, Farewell!".

Unfortunately, Joaquin died of cardiac arrest in the early morning of April 29, 2004, at his home in San Juan, Metro Manila. He was then editor of Philippine Graphic magazine, where he worked with Juan P. Dayang, the magazine's first publisher. Joaquin was also publisher of its sister publication, Mirror Weekly, a women's magazine, and wrote the column “Small Beer” for the Philippine Daily Inquirer and Isyu, an opinion tabloid.

Education

Nick Joaquin, a former teacher, dropped out of Mapa High School in his third year due to the confined classroom environment and preferred learning outside. He worked as an apprentice in a bakery in Pasay and later at the publishing company TVT, which provided him with a small taste of the industry he would spend most of his life in. Joaquin was an avid reader, with his sister-in-law, Sarah K. Joaquin, describing him as having a "rabid and insane love for books."

His parents encouraged his interest in books from a young age, and he had a borrower's card at the National Library when he was ten. He read extensively, enjoying the works of Edna St. Vincent Millay, Vachel Lindsay, Anton Chekhov, Dostoyevsky, D. H. Lawrence, and Willa Cather. He also read American magazines and discovered the fiction of Booth Tarkington, Somerset Maugham, F. Scott Fitzgerald, and Ernest Hemingway.

Career

At 17, Joaquin published his first English poem about Don Quixote in the pre-World War II Tribune, where he worked as a proofreader. He identified with the character and later used the pen names Quijano de Pacó and Quijano de Manila. In 1937, he published his first short story, "The Sorrows of Vaudeville," in the Sunday Tribune Magazine.

After winning a nationwide essay competition to honor La Naval de Manila, sponsored by the Dominican Order, Joaquin was awarded an honorary Associate in Arts (A.A.) and a scholarship to St. Albert's Convent, the Dominican monastery in Hong Kong. He returned to Manila after two years in Hong Kong.

Joaquin continued publishing stories and poems between 1934 and 1941 in the Herald Mid-Week Magazine and the Sunday Tribune Magazine. The Commonwealth years were a vibrant era in Philippine literature, but the Japanese occupation closed down the Tribune and other publications. Despite the occupation, Joaquin continued writing, with his work appearing in the Philippine Review in 1943. His story "It Was Later Than We Thought" and translation of Rizal's Mi Ultimo Adios were published, sparking interest from readers. However, he shied away from recognition, creating a mysterious and distant author.

In 1957, Joaquin represented the Philippines at the International PEN Congress and was appointed as a member of the Motion Pictures Commission. He was honored as a National Artist and worked for intellectual freedom, securing the release of imprisoned writer José F. Lacaba. Joaquin delivered an invocation to Maria Makiling, a diwata and the mountain's mythical maiden, at a ceremony attended by First Lady Imelda Marcos. After returning to the Philippines, he joined the Philippines Free Press and gained attention for his poems, stories, plays, and journalism under the pen name Quijano de Manila. His intellectual and provocative journalism raised the country's level of reporting.

Awards

Don Carlos Palanca Memorial Literary Awards 1957, 1958, 1965 and 1976

The Don Carlos Palanca Memorial Awards for Literature, popularly known as the Palanca Awards, are a set of literary awards for Philippine writers. Usually referred to as the "Pulitzer Prize of the Philippines," it is the country's highest literary honor in terms of prestige.

Ramon Magsaysay Award for Journalism, Literature, and Creative Communication Arts 1996

The Ramon Magsaysay Award (Filipino: Gawad Ramon Magsaysay) is an annual award established to perpetuate former Philippine President Ramon Magsaysay's example of integrity in governance, courageous service to the people, and pragmatic idealism within a democratic society.

National Artist Award 1976

The order of the highest state honor is conferred on individuals deemed as having done much for their artistic field. Deserving individuals must have been recommended by both the Cultural Center and the National Commission for Culture and the Arts prior to receiving the Award. Such people are then titled, by virtue of a Presidential Proclamation, as National Artist (Filipino: Gawad Pambansang Alagad ng Sining), and are inducted into the Order.

Legacy

Literary Contributions

Nick Joaquin's literary works have left an indelible mark on Philippine literature. His mastery of the English language and his exploration of Filipino culture and history have inspired generations of writers.

Cultural Impact

Through his stories, plays, and essays, Joaquin shed light on various aspects of Filipino culture, traditions, and societal issues. He played a significant role in shaping the Filipino identity and preserving cultural heritage.

Inspiration to Future Generations

Nick Joaquin's legacy extends beyond his literary achievements. He serves as a source of inspiration for aspiring writers, intellectuals, and cultural advocates who seek to make a positive impact on Philippine society.
